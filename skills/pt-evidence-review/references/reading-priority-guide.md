# Reading Priority Guide

Use this reference when multiple candidate papers have been identified and the user needs a practical reading order.

## Goal

Rank papers for first-pass reading based on how much they help answer the user's clinical question quickly and credibly.

## Default priority order

1. Direct systematic reviews or meta-analyses on the same condition, intervention, and main outcome
2. Recent RCTs closest to the user's patient, timing, and intervention format
3. Older or foundational RCTs that still directly test the question
4. Non-randomized comparative studies that add context
5. Indirect, mixed-population, or support-only papers

## What makes a paper high priority

- The population closely matches the user's patient or clinical setting.
- The intervention resembles what the therapist could actually deliver.
- The study addresses the user's main outcome directly.
- The paper is likely to change practice or sharpen the next clinical decision.
- The design offers relatively strong evidence for the question.

## What lowers priority

- Mixed populations where the target group is only one subgroup
- Different postoperative phase from the user's actual case
- Intervention too different from routine PT practice
- Outcomes that do not address the user's main concern
- Older evidence that has likely been superseded by newer reviews

## Practical tie-breakers

If two papers look similar, prefer:

- the paper with more direct clinical matching
- the review before the single trial
- the newer paper when methods and directness are comparable
- the trial with clearer intervention details when planning application

## Journal club reading pattern

When the user wants efficient journal club preparation, recommend:

1. Read one direct review first for the big picture.
2. Read one or two direct RCTs next for concrete methods and outcomes.
3. Use indirect or mixed-population papers only if they add something missing.

## Output preference

When using this reference, present:

- ranked list
- one-sentence reason for each rank
- quick recommendation for which one or two papers to read first

## Reminder

Directness to the clinical question usually matters more than publication year alone.
